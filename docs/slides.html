Simulation Environment

One simulated week at a SaaS company. An agent is the PM; simulated coworkers do the work.

1 · the world

Time jumps between events — nothing else exists

Mon 09:05 you ping Sarah ← your action costs 1 sim-minute Mon 09:06 you skip ahead 3 hours… Mon 09:42 …interrupted: Sarah replies ← her latency: a seeded 5–45 min draw Mon 09:42 the model thinks for 30 real seconds — sim clock doesn't move Tue 09:00 overnight, the migration gained 0 hours: nobody works at night — progress is computed from the calendar, no process ever "runs"

Everything lands in one append-only event log → any instant can be rebuilt exactly, with zero LLM calls. And nothing is polled: chats wake you mid-skip; email arrives when the next inbox-check event fires.

1 · the world — communication

Three channels, three price tags

chat 10:00 ping Sarah 10:01 ping again 10:02 ping again → she answers in 5–45 min → but loses 3 × 20 min of deep-work focus

interruptions stack — they don't overlap

email 10:00 email Sarah 11:17 she reads it at her next inbox pass → slower answer → zero focus cost

async by social contract; the agent's own inbox check fires every 30 min

meeting 14:00 1h sync, 4 people → instant answers, minutes kept → costs the org 4 person-hours

the most expensive artifact in any company

None of these numbers appear in any tool description. Agents learn them from timestamps, from people (the cost is felt — over-pinged coworkers complain in character), or from reward: noisy runs simply score lower.

1 · the world — why agents can't just pick one channel

Each channel has a scenario it strictly wins

chat wins Fri 09:30 Sarah is blocked NOW on a one-line answer you have. chat → unblocked in ≤45 min 20-min tax ≪ hours blocked email → sits ~2h in her inbox the morning is gone meet → calendars + a slot: the priciest way to say one sentence
email wins Thu 09:00 execs want sign-off notes from BOTH engineers by end of day. No rush. email → zero focus cost, answered at next pass — hours of slack to spare chat×2 → 40+ min of focus stolen mid-crunch → completions slip meet → 3 person-hours for something async
meeting wins Wed the migration IS the critical path; Sarah alone misses Friday. 2h session on the task: 1.3 + 0.6×1.0 = 1.9× → 3.8h banked → ships Thu chat/email → words move, work doesn't — no channel adds hands but a session

This is the reward-hacking defense: no channel is decoration and none dominates — a policy that always chats, always emails, or always meets is strictly beaten somewhere, and the physics (not a judge) hands out the loss. Measured: the always-chatting PM scores below zero; the channel-matching PM approaches the ceiling.

1 · the world — tasks

New work lands on someone — usually the wrong someone

Tue 11:30 incident arrives → auto-assigned to Dave (the yes-to-everything volunteer) Tue 11:30 Dave pings you: "SSO is down for Meridian — I've got it 👍" (ticket: sso-4f2a, currently on dave) Dave's queue: 30h. Sarah frees up Wednesday. your move A: Tue 11:32 reassign → Sarah → ships Thu 15:00 your move B: Thu 09:00 reassign → Sarah → work only counts from Thursday → misses Friday your move C: do nothing → Dave gets to it eventually → the unmanaged week

Coworkers self-schedule sensibly (highest priority first, switching when that changes, half-done work never lost). The PM's levers: move a task, reorder a queue (scheduling only — relabeling priority can't inflate the score), or leave the defaults alone. Option C — the fully unmanaged week — is the score's zero point.

2 · belief updates

Every fact exists three times

the migration, Monday 09:05: truth 55% done ← only the engine & the grader can see this belief Sarah: "70%, on track" ← honest. wrong. she hasn't re-checked. the board "70%, on track" ← inherited her stale claim ask her Monday → you get an honest 70%. there is no oracle to interrogate. and Dave insists: "rate-limiting is launch-critical!" ← authored red herring; it isn't. chasing it burns real capacity.

Beliefs mature on an authored schedule (deterministic events, no LLM on any information path). The board self-corrects only on completions — the one public, reliable signal in the environment.

2 · belief updates — one task's week

What the agent can know, and when

Mon 09:05 board: "~70%, on track" Sarah believes it. truth: 55%. Tue 14:00 belief-update event fires → Sarah realizes 55% she pings you, unprompted: "schema rework surfaced — ~18 focused hours left" Wed 10:00 you ask again → her honest current belief (still not truth) Thu 16:30 migration actually completes → board flips to Done two completions ≈ a speed estimate for that person

The graded skill is not truth-extraction — it's operating under honest noise: keep slack against estimates, react fast to confessions, calibrate people from what ships.

3 · skills

Some people are quietly faster — nobody tells you

config (frozen numbers, never a runtime LLM): sarah.skills = {db: 1.3} dave.skills = {auth: 1.3} the same 12h database task: on Dave → 12h of calendar on Sarah → 9¼h (12 ÷ 1.3)
what the agent sees: board: nothing about skills tools: nothing about skills what the agent can notice: Mon gave Sarah the db task Thu she finished a day "early" → infer she's the db specialist → route the next one accordingly

Identical physics for every rollout of a task (clean group-relative advantages), and nothing an agent can phrase its way into. The scoring ceiling routes by skill too — so "give the db task to the db person" is part of what a perfect week means.

3 · skills — combined in working sessions

Meetings can move work — boundedly

migration is the bottleneck (Sarah's, db). book a working session ON it: schedule_meeting( attendees=[sarah, dave], task="migration", 2h) rate = 1.3 (sarah, best) + 0.6 × 1.0 (dave) = 1.9× → 2h room ⇒ 3.8h of progress, banked when the meeting ends
the fine print: helpers add 1, 0.6, 0.36, … of their rate — capped at 2.5× a 6-person pile-on ≈ a 3-person session. never linear in headcount. the bill: Dave's own 30h queue is frozen for those 2 hours.

“Adding manpower to a late software project makes it later.” — Fred Brooks, The Mythical Man-Month (1975). The cap and the diminishing weights are that law written as physics: the winning move is swarming skill-matched people on the critical path — and nothing else.

4 · the rubrics

Every task instance carries its own scale

< 0 made the week worse
pings, churn, busywork
0 = the unmanaged week
defaults play out, agent does nothing
1 = the perfect week
frictionless optimum, exhaustive search
one generated instance (both anchors computed from the config file, before any agent runs): unmanaged week ships 58% of the work-weight → score 0 perfect week ships 100%, early → score 1 an agent ships 79%, mostly early → score 0.41 ← the fraction of the gap captured

The gap's width IS the authored difficulty — it measures how wrong the week's default assignments are. A generator remixes physics (efforts, timings, default owners, ticket ids) under seeded draws; gates reject anything invalid, unfair, or degenerate.
