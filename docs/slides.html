the task file

A whole task is one config file — every knob is a number

{ "company": "Meridian — 6-engineer launch team", "start_time": Mon 09:00, "costs": { chat_interrupt_minutes: 10 }, ← the focus tax "physics": { parallel_cap: 1.5, swarm_cap: 2.5 }, ← pooling (Brooks), tunable "answer_batch_minutes": 120, ← how long an email answer waits "tasks": [{ id: api-core, priority: P0, effort_h: 60, done_h: 18, assignees: [sam], tags: [backend], blocked_by: [design-specs], belief: [{ remaining_frac: .15, held_by: sam }, Mon: "nearly done" { at: Tue 13:00, remaining_frac: .70, ping }], the slip questions: [{ at: Tue 09:00, gates: true, held_by: sam, gates ⇒ the task ping: "OAuth or email+pw? blocked till you call it" }] }], STALLS till you answer "arrivals": [{ at: Tue, task: { security-audit, P1, assignees:[maya] }}], ← wrong (busy) owner "npcs": [{ id: sam, skills: { backend: 1.9 } }], ← HIDDEN speed; never shown "band": [ no-PM baseline , OPT ] ← stamped by the gate, pre-run }

Nothing is code. Difficulty, hidden skills, the belief slip, the blocking decision, the wrong default owner — each is a number a generator can remix, and the same seed replays the week byte-for-byte. The scoring band is computed from this file before any agent runs.

1 · the world — communication

Three channels, three price tags

chat 10:00 ping Sarah 10:01 ping again 10:02 ping again → answers in 5–45 min (seeded delay) → each ping costs a 10-min refocus tax (chat_interrupt_minutes), and they STACK: 3 × 10 = 30 min lost

the tax is a config knob; taxes stack, they don't overlap

email 10:00 email Sarah 11:17 she reads it at her next inbox pass → slower answer → zero focus cost

answered at her next inbox pass; the agent's own inbox batches every 30 min (email_batch_minutes, config)

meeting 14:00 1h sync, 4 people → instant answers, minutes kept → 1h × 4 people off their own work = 4 person-hours

the most expensive artifact in any company

Every number here is a config knob (the taxes/batches) or a seeded draw (reply delays) — none appear in any tool description. Agents learn them three ways: timestamps, complaints from over-pinged coworkers, or lower scores.

1 · the world — why agents can't just pick one channel

Each channel has a scenario it strictly wins

chat wins Fri 09:30 Sarah's task is STALLED until you answer one question. chat → reply lands at once → unblocked. 20-min tax ≪ the hours she'd sit idle email → waits ~2h for her inbox → the morning is gone meet → a room booked to say one sentence
email wins Thu 09:00 execs want sign-off notes from BOTH engineers by end of day. No rush. email → zero focus cost, answered at next pass — hours of slack to spare chat×2 → 40+ min of focus stolen mid-crunch → completions slip meet → 3 person-hours for something async
meeting wins Wed migration is the critical path; Sarah alone misses Fri. 2h working session, Sarah+Dave: rule: extra hands fade — 1st ×1.0, 2nd ×0.6, 3rd ×0.36 … (coordination overhead, Brooks) 1st hand Sarah 1.0 × 1.3 = 1.3 full weight × her db skill 2nd hand Dave 0.6 × 1.0 = 0.6 fade 0.6 × his 1.0 (no db) pooled = 1.3 + 0.6 = 1.9× banked = 2h × 1.9 = 3.8h at meeting-end → ships Thu
chat/email move words, not work — only a room adds a 2nd pair of hands.

No channel always wins → no one-channel shortcut survives. The loss comes from physics, not a judge. Measured: an always-chatting PM scores below zero.

1 · the world — tasks

New work lands on someone — usually the wrong someone

Tue 11:30 incident arrives → auto-assigned to Dave (the yes-to-everything volunteer) Tue 11:30 Dave pings you: "SSO is down for Meridian — I've got it 👍" (ticket: sso-4f2a, currently on dave) Dave's queue: 30h. Sarah frees up Wednesday. your move A: Tue 11:32 reassign → Sarah → ships Thu 15:00 your move B: Thu 09:00 reassign → Sarah → work only counts from Thursday → misses Friday your move C: do nothing → Dave gets to it eventually → the unmanaged week

People self-schedule by priority; half-done work is never lost. Your levers: move a task or reorder a queue (relabeling can't inflate the score). Option C — the unmanaged week — is the score's zero point.

the coworkers

Each coworker is an agent: a persona + private knowledge

npc maya { role: "Backend Lead", persona: "direct, but guarded about her own slippage; only comes clean when asked a specific question", knowledge: [ "you own the API; 3 people idle behind you until you lock it", "you're blocked on a data-model call only the PM can make" ], skills: { backend: 1.9 } ← hidden }

An LLM voices them — but they have no write channel: their words never mutate the world, only rules do. So NPC text can't be injected to change physics or score, and runs stay deterministic.

What makes them feel real: proactive outreach (they ping you when something breaks), realistic reply delays, and honesty shaped by persona — Maya hedges until you ask the right question; Dev over-shares a herring. You infer the truth from what they do (completions), not what they say.

Persona + private knowledge are authored in the config; each NPC's experience stream (what they've seen) is appended as the week unfolds — a pure, replayable fold, no hidden RNG.

2 · belief updates

Every fact exists three times

the migration, Monday 09:05 — three different numbers: truth 55% done ← reality. only the engine & the grader see it. belief Sarah: "70%" ← what she'd tell you — honest, but she hasn't re-checked. the board "60%" ← the last status anyone logged: stale, its own number. ask her Monday → you get her honest 70% (not the truth). there is no oracle to interrogate. and Dave insists: "rate-limiting is launch-critical!" ← authored red herring; it isn't. chasing it burns real capacity.

Belief changes are scheduled events — no LLM touches information. The board self-corrects only on completions, the one reliable public signal.

2 · belief updates — one task's week

What the agent can know, and when

Mon 09:05 Sarah's estimate: "~8h left" honest. wrong. truth: ~36h. Tue 14:00 belief-update event fires → Sarah realizes it's ~36h she pings you, unprompted: "the SDK integration blew up — ~36h left, not 8" Wed 10:00 you ask again → her honest current estimate (still not truth) Thu 16:30 migration actually completes → board flips to Done two completions ≈ a speed estimate for that person

Estimates are in hours-left, not %, and the blind spot belongs to the person: reassign the task to a fresh owner and Sarah's stale number doesn't follow. The graded skill — keep slack, react fast to confessions, calibrate people from what actually ships.

3 · skills

Some people are quietly faster — nobody tells you

config (frozen numbers, never a runtime LLM): sarah.skills = {db: 1.3} dave.skills = {auth: 1.3} the same 12h database task: on Dave → 12h of calendar on Sarah → 9¼h (12 ÷ 1.3)
what the agent sees: board: nothing about skills tools: nothing about skills what the agent can notice: Mon gave Sarah the db task Thu she finished a day "early" → infer she's the db specialist → route the next one accordingly

Same physics for every rollout — stable rewards, nothing an agent can talk its way into. The perfect week routes by skill, so discovering specialists is part of the job.

3 · skills — more than one person on a task

Two ways to add hands to a task — parallel, or a meeting

migration is the bottleneck (Sarah's, db). Sarah solo → 1.3×. two ways to add Dave: 1) parallel — no meeting → 1.5× both work it at once but uncoordinated: conflict & duplicate, so the 2nd hand adds only a little (Brooks). 2) a 2h working session → 1.9× the meeting SYNCS them (1.3 + 0.6×1.0), banked at meeting-end — hands land.

Both help; the meeting buys the jump from 1.5 to 1.9 — paid for in everyone's time in the room.

the rule — capped-additive pooling: c = each person's skill on the task's tags (geomean; 1.0 if no skill there) sort high→low: c₁ ≥ c₂ ≥ … rate = c₁ + 0.6·c₂ + 0.36·c₃ + … then cap: parallel 1.5, meeting 2.5 so Sarah(1.3)+Dave(1.0): 1.3 + 0.6·1.0 = 1.9 (meeting) ; capped at 1.5 (parallel) the bill (either way): every helper's own queue is frozen. meetings are capped PER PERSON: ≤ 180 min/day, and none over an out-of-office block — can't swarm people who aren't there.

“Adding manpower to a late software project makes it later.” — Fred Brooks, The Mythical Man-Month (1975). The two caps and the fading weights are that law as physics.

4 · the rubrics

Every task instance carries its own scale

< 0 made the week worse
pings, churn, busywork
0 = the unmanaged week
defaults play out, agent does nothing
1 = best solo week
one owner per task, zero friction — a reference, not a ceiling
both anchors computed from the config file — before any agent runs: unmanaged week ships 58% of the work-weight → score 0 best solo week ships 100%, early → score 1 (one owner/task) an agent ships 79%, mostly early → score 0.41 score > 1 is real, not a bug — the agent out-coordinated the solo optimum: pulling idle teammates onto the bottleneck (parallel work, or a session) — a lever a one-owner schedule can't use. bounded by the caps. never clamped.

Why an unbeatable ceiling isn't needed: the score is an affine map of COMBINED, and GRPO advantages are affine-invariant (next slide) — so 1 only has to be a cheap, stable anchor. The gap's width IS the difficulty: how wrong the week's defaults are.

4 · the rubrics — the two anchors

Both anchors come from the config — before any agent runs

BASELINE (no-PM floor → score 0) for each arrival: owner ← its org-fallback pickup run the SAME scheduler, zero PM actions value ← Σ w·(progress + done, early)

an honest unmanaged week — the team self-organizes, badly. Not a strawman.

OPT (frictionless best → score 1) for assignment in owners^tasks: exhaustive # greedy + hill-climb once too big run SAME scheduler, busy={}, questions answered at t0, true skills value ← max combined over all

a cheap, stable reference — not an unbeatable ceiling (coordination can pass it).

SCORE = (agent − baseline) / (OPT − baseline) both fold the SAME scheduler over config TRUTH — never beliefs; pure functions of the file, stamped once
4 · the rubrics — complexity levers

Difficulty is seven dials — pull any axis, the week hardens there

■ an easy week   ■ a hard week — same seven axes, pulled further out. A generated instance is one such shape.

capacity pressure — slack disappears; every misstep costs a deliverable default-owner wrongness — arrivals land on the busiest; rearranging = the job dependency chains — one slip cascades down the critical path arrival timing & channel — later ambush, buried in email; reaction window shrinks belief error & confession — Monday's picture more wrong, truth lands later skill spread — hidden specialists diverge; misrouting costs more forced triage — work > capacity; choosing what dies is the skill

Every lever is a number in a config file, so the band is programmable: a tuning loop widens it (more winnable) or sharpens/spreads it (which decisions it teaches) by editing one seed number and re-measuring — crunch → mobile → bigweek walked winnable 7 → 10 → 26 this way. One bound on all of them: a task must stay completable from the moment the PM could first know of it.

4 · the rubrics — what is measured

Outcomes only — and we can measure what it teaches

COMPLETION = Σ w·(progress + α·done)/(1+α) the work got done EFFICIENCY = Σ w·done·(horizon − done_at)/span …and landed early COMBINED = (COMPLETION + γ·EFFICIENCY)/(1+γ) the reward scalar FAIRNESS = 1 − σ(hours worked / available) team health, per person SCORE = (agent − baseline)/(OPT − baseline) position between the anchors = a·COMBINED + b, a > 0 fixed per task an affine transform

wauthored priority weight — P0 8 · P1 4 · P2 2 · P3 1 (agent-created tasks: w = 0)

GRPO's group normalization (r − mean)/std is invariant to affine transforms — within one task, SCORE and raw COMBINED yield identical advantages. The normalization changes nothing for training; its job is to put hundreds of tasks on one scale.

scope — built incrementally, on evidence

What's deliberately not modelled (yet) — and why

PRINCIPLED EXCLUSIONS (kept out by design — determinism & reward-hacking resistance) • communication content is ungraded a clear vs sloppy doc score the same; words earn nothing — only outcomes do • NPCs have no free agency they don't refuse / negotiate / quit off-script — behaviour is config-keyed, else determinism & replay break • no LLM on the physics / scoring path
ROADMAP — next increments (each ships only once an ablation shows it owns real band-share) • scope levers: descope / split a task, negotiate the deadline • quality & rework: rushing seeds a rework task — speed-vs-quality • partial availability → cross-project & multi-week horizons (OOO = step 1) • close the generate→measure→adjust loop to hit a target difficulty tier

Built layer by layer, not 0→100: a mechanic is added only after it's measured to move the reward — and kept honest when it doesn't (coordination/pooling is in, but the ablation shows it's situational, not a headline lever). Knowing what's out is how you benchmark cleanly.
